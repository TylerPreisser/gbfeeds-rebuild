The most common question I get after someone puts out their first bag of Buck Chow is: what's in this? You can read the guaranteed analysis panel on the back of the bag. But reading "crude protein min. 20.0%" doesn't tell you much unless you know what's providing that protein and why we chose it.

This is the walk I do in my head every time I look at a batch. Every ingredient in Buck Chow has a reason to be there. If it doesn't serve the deer, it doesn't make the cut.

## Corn

Start with the base. Corn is the highest-energy grain you can feed deer. It's not a protein source — at 8–9% crude protein, corn doesn't move the needle — but it's the energy platform everything else rides on. Deer need dense carbohydrate energy, especially going into and through the rut when bucks are covering 10–15 miles a day and burning through body reserves.

The corn in Buck Chow is processed and blended at the right ratio. Too much corn and you've got an attractant that deer will gorge on without the protein and mineral support they actually need. Not enough and you lose the palatability that brings deer in from distance.

Corn is also the component most hunters already understand. When you see deer hammering a Buck Chow site the same way they'd hit a corn pile, you're watching the corn do exactly what it's supposed to: get them to the site and keep them coming back.

## Soybean Meal

This is where the 20% protein number comes from. Soybean meal is the primary protein engine in Buck Chow, and it's one of the most digestible protein sources you can put in front of a whitetail.

There's a difference between crude protein percentage and digestible protein. Some feeds hit a high crude protein number by using lower-quality, less-digestible sources that pass through the rumen without being fully absorbed. Roasted soybean meal has a high bypass protein fraction — a portion of the protein passes the rumen and is absorbed directly in the small intestine. That bypass fraction is critical for muscle and antler tissue building, especially during peak velvet growth when a buck needs to manufacture up to an inch of antler per day.

If you pulled the soybean meal out of Buck Chow and replaced it with a cheaper protein source to hold the same crude protein percentage on paper, you'd see the antler response drop. Not because the number changed — because the quality of what the deer actually absorbed changed.

## Oats

Oats are in Buck Chow for two reasons: energy and palatability.

Oats carry a significant fat content compared to most other grains — roughly 5% crude fat — and fat is the highest-density energy source available to a ruminant. During late winter when does are in early gestation and bucks are rebuilding body weight lost through the rut, that fat energy is what keeps them in condition when forage quality drops.

The second reason is texture. Oats add a rougher, more fibrous component to the mix that bucks find attractive. I've watched deer at Buck Chow sites select for the oat fraction, sorting through the pile. That selection behavior is good — it slows their intake rate and forces them to stay at the site longer, which is what you want from a conditioning standpoint.

## Molasses

Molasses is the binding agent and the long-range aroma driver. When you open a bag of Buck Chow and smell it from six feet away, that's the molasses you're smelling.

Deer have approximately 297 million olfactory receptors in their nose compared to a human's 5 million. That's not a small difference. A deer can detect and follow a scent trail from hundreds of yards. Molasses gives Buck Chow a dense, sweet, penetrating aroma that carries downwind — exactly what you need to pull deer into a new site from distance.

From a nutrition standpoint, molasses adds simple sugars that are rapidly absorbed, giving a quick energy hit layered on top of the sustained-release energy from corn and oats. It's a minor nutritional contributor but it's important: the sugar makes the feed immediately rewarding, which builds the habit of returning to the site.

## Calcium and Phosphorus Minerals

You'll see "Calcium min. 0.6%, Phosphorus min. 0.4%" on the guaranteed analysis panel. Those numbers look small. They aren't.

Calcium and phosphorus are the two primary structural minerals in antler tissue. A mature buck's antlers are roughly 22% calcium and 11% phosphorus by weight. During peak velvet growth, a buck is depositing calcium and phosphorus into antler tissue faster than almost any biological process in the mammalian world.

The problem is that natural forage — grass, browse, agricultural residue — rarely provides enough of either mineral during the peak demand window of April through August. Properties that run year-round mineral programs through Antler Growth consistently produce heavier, denser antlers than properties that don't, even when the deer populations are similar in age structure and genetics.

Buck Chow's mineral inclusion is designed for year-round feeding. The ratios are set to complement typical forage mineral levels, not to overdose. You can feed Buck Chow twelve months a year without worrying about excess mineral intake — the formulation is calibrated for continuous use.

## Why Everything Is There

The formula isn't complicated. Corn for energy and palatability. Soybean meal for high-quality digestible protein. Oats for fat energy and texture. Molasses for aroma and taste. Calcium and phosphorus for antler and herd health.

There is nothing in Buck Chow that doesn't have a job. No fillers, no cheap grain substitutes, no binders added to make the numbers look better on paper.

The reason Buck Chow produces the results it does — over 7,500 inches documented in 2023 and 2024 across Kansas properties — is that every ingredient is working at the same time. Attraction brings deer to the site. Protein and minerals do the work once they're there. Energy keeps them coming back.

That's the formula. Every bag.
