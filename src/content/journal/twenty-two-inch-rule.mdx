We say "7,500 inches" on the website. Customers ask me what that means. How are you counting? What counts as a harvest inch? Who's verifying this?

Fair questions. Here's the complete answer.

## The 22-Inch Rule

A mature whitetail buck's ears, spread tip to tip, measure roughly 16–18 inches depending on the deer and the region. Kansas deer run toward the larger end of that range — we see 17–18 inches tip-to-tip on most mature animals.

The 22-inch rule works like this: if a buck's antlers break outside the ear tips on both sides, he's carrying at least a 19-inch outside spread. If they break by a noticeable margin — meaning you can see daylight between the main beam and the ear tip — he's probably at 21–23 inches. If his antlers are clearly past his ears to the point where you'd have to estimate where they end, that's a 23-inch-plus animal.

Twenty-two inches outside spread is not a scoring number. It's a field judgment number. It tells you whether to pick up your bow before the deer steps into your shooting lane. You can make that judgment in about three seconds while the deer is 60 yards out in thick timber. A formal Boone and Crockett panel score takes hours.

The 22-inch outside spread number correlates pretty tightly with gross B&C scores in the 130–145 range for a typical 8-point frame, depending on mass, tine length, and symmetry. Those are the deer we're talking about when we say GB Feeds helped produce a trophy animal.

## What We Count — and What We Don't

When a customer sends in a harvest report, here's what counts toward the running total:

**Main beam length counts.** Both sides, inside-tine to tip. This is the single largest contributor to a final score. A Kansas 8-point with 24-inch main beams is carrying 48 inches before you measure a single tine.

**Tine length counts.** All six tines on a standard 8-point (G1 through G4 on each side, though most hunters count G2 through G4 as the primary tines). The G2, or brow tine, and the G3 are typically the longest and are the primary scoring tines.

**Inside spread credit counts.** This is one number, not two — it's the inside distance between the main beams at their widest point.

**What doesn't count:** kickers, sticker points, and any non-typical growth that a hunter can't reliably measure in the field. We're not running an official scoring system. We're tracking real inches that real hunters are reporting from real animals they harvested using our feed.

## Why We Use a Conservative Number

Our website says "over 7,500 inches." The actual running total in our customer logs is higher. We use the conservative number for a reason.

When hunters self-report scores, there's natural variation in how measurements are taken. Some guys are running a tape measure. Some are eyeballing. Some are scoring a green animal in the field in November and haven't done a formal panel score. We use a conservative baseline because we'd rather say 7,500 and know we're right than say 10,000 and have a handful of those inches be generous estimates.

The point of the stat isn't to impress people with the biggest number we can put on a website. The point is to demonstrate, with a defensible figure, that GB Feeds customers are harvesting trophy-class animals at a rate that stands up to scrutiny.

## Why Greens Don't Count

"Green" scoring means scoring an antler in the 60 days immediately following the harvest, before the antler has fully dried. Antler is approximately 60% mineral and 40% protein when it's fresh. As it dries, it loses moisture and shrinks slightly. A green score is typically 3–7 inches higher than the dried final score.

We don't count green scores in our running total because we're trying to document real antler production, not dehydration artifacts. When a customer tells me their deer scored 165 green in November, I'm happy for them. I'm not putting 165 in the log. I'll take 158 after 60 days of drying.

This is consistent with how Boone and Crockett and Pope and Young handle it. The 60-day drying period is standard because the organizations recognized early that green scoring inflated records and made comparison between measurements taken at different times meaningless.

## How This Connects to What We're Doing

The 7,500-inch number is meaningful because of how we're counting, not in spite of it. Every inch in that total is from a dried, field-verified harvest by a real customer who used our products.

It's not a marketing number we generated by counting loosely. It's what happened on Kansas properties when hunters used Buck Chow the way it was designed to be used — year-round, consistently, starting in spring before the velvet window opens.

When you see a buck you've been watching since August, and he shows up in October with the frames you've been hoping for, that's the return on a spring and summer feeding program. That's the inch count working the way it's supposed to work.
