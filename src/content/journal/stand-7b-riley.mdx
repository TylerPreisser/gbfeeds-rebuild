Stand 7B sits on the east edge of a creek drainage in Riley County, maybe sixty yards off a property line, facing a cut cornfield that hasn't been plowed in four years. It's one of those stands that should be perfect: good wind corridor, natural funnel, water nearby. For two seasons it was producing. Then October 2022 happened.

The spin feeder I had running there — a 600-pound unit I'd been filling with Buck Chow since August — threw a motor bearing on October 8th. I didn't know it was down for eight days. By the time I drove out to check it, I'd already had two sits over the site with zero deer activity. I pulled the cameras and looked at what happened the week prior.

The bucks were there on October 1st. Gone by October 4th. And they didn't come back when I fixed the feeder on October 16th.

## What Actually Happened

A spin feeder operates on a timer. When the motor fails, the timer keeps ticking but nothing comes out. Deer aren't stupid — they learn the pattern. When feed stops appearing at the scheduled times, they move on to the next consistent source.

With a gravity feeder, there's no motor to fail. Feed flows when deer want it. The only failure mode is running out of feed, which I can manage by filling it on a schedule I control.

I've thought about this a lot over the past two years. The spin feeder model is built for feeding corn on a game management schedule. It's not built for Buck Chow and the kind of consistent, all-day attraction that a high-protein feed site needs to work.

Buck Chow is designed to be available when deer want protein, not when a timer says they can have it. The 20% protein content, the roasted soybeans, the mineral profile — those things do their best work when deer can self-regulate their intake. You can't manage that with a timed spin cycle.

## The Switch

After that season I moved Stand 7B to a TWS 600-pound gravity feeder. Filled it September 10th, 2023. By September 17th I had a mature 8-point on camera that I'd never seen before on this property.

The difference isn't just the feeder type. It's the consistency. Gravity feeders don't have off-days. They don't have motor failures, dead batteries, or a missed timer window. They work until they're empty.

I fill mine every four to five weeks during the season. September through January, that's six trips to the stand. The feeder stays loaded, the site stays active, and deer pattern onto it without interruption.

## What the Pre-Rut Window Needs

October 8th — the date of that original failure — is right in the pre-rut window for Riley County. Bucks in my area start breaking their late-summer patterns around October 5th. They're expanding their range, refreshing scrapes from last year, and their nose is running ahead of their body. That's exactly when you need your feed site to be maximally consistent.

A site going dark for even three days during the first two weeks of October can break a pattern that took six weeks to build.

This year, Stand 7B ran continuous from September 8th through November 15th. I had the mature 8-point I'd been watching since August come through at 7:22 PM on October 14th, on his way from the creek drainage to a doe bedding area I'd identified in August.

He was exactly where I expected him to be, doing exactly what I expected him to do, because the feed site had been consistent enough to keep him in his pattern instead of pushing him off his range looking for a reliable source.

## The Practical Upside Nobody Talks About

Here's something I don't hear mentioned enough: gravity feeders are dramatically easier to work with during the season. No batteries to check. No motors to listen to. You pull up, check the level, fill it if needed, reset the camera, and leave.

With a spin feeder, every service trip involves checking the motor, testing the timer, making sure the spinner arm isn't clogged. That's 10–15 minutes of extra time at a stand you're trying not to burn. Every minute you spend dinking with equipment is time you're putting scent into the area.

At Stand 7B, I'm in and out in under eight minutes. Every time.

## One Caveat on Spin Feeders

I'm not saying spin feeders don't work. For corn, for timed supplemental feeding, for certain property configurations — they have their place. If you're running a large property with multiple sites and you need precise scheduling to manage feed across all of them, a spin feeder gives you that control.

But for a dedicated Buck Chow site with a high-protein, high-attraction feed, especially during the pre-rut and rut, gravity wins. The consistency is the product.
